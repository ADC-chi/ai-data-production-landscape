Abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) depends on data production: the sociotechnical process that transforms human knowledge into computational resources. The connections among AI systems, data practices, and impacts on Indigenous, underrepresented, and underserved communities‚Äîthough critical‚Äîhave not been systematically examined.

To this end, we conduct a Multivocal Literature Review (MLR) integrating 350 academic and grey-literature sources. Across five analytic domains, we distinguish extractive data production mechanisms that centralize control from high-agency pathways in which communities exercise authority.

Key Contributions

  1. Reframing Data Production: Positioning it as a site of sociotechnical power rather than a technical prerequisite.
  2. Design Implications: Proposing upstream infrastructure, provenance-first architectures, and federated governance.
  3. Methodological Validation: Demonstrating multivocal synthesis to bridge academic critique with community practice.
  4. Open Corpus: A mapped database of sources across pipeline stages, historical eras, and geographic contexts.

Analytic Domains: Extractive vs. High-Agency

We distinguish between extractive mechanisms that centralize control and high-agency pathways where communities exercise authority.

Domain ‚ö†Ô∏è Extractive Pattern ‚úÖ High-Agency Pathway
Data Relations
Authority & Consent		 Outside agenda-setting; bundled consent. Co-authored scope; granular & revocable consent.
Data Labor
Work & Credit		 Invisible work; piece-work; no share of value. Attributed labor; context pay; value-sharing.
Data Representation
Legibility & Context		 External taxonomies; category erasure. Community ontologies; context-preserving schemas; refusal.
Data Infrastructure
Storage & Flow		 Centralized silos; opaque lineage; one-way flows. Provenance-first storage; tiered access; federated spaces.
Data Governance
Rights & Redress		 Rights-washing; non-consensual reuse; weak redress. Community licenses; sovereign oversight; enforceable refusal.

Taxonomy of Practices

This project synthesizes extractive patterns and less-extractive pathways identified across the literature.

‚ö†Ô∏è Extractive Patterns

  • Excluding underrepresented groups from decision-making
  • Collecting vast amounts of data to train AI systems
  • Reproducing biases through synthetic data generation
  • Scraping or repurposing sensitive data
  • Prioritizing data wants over community needs
  • Soliciting data without reciprocal benefits
  • Exploitative and invisible data labor
  • Biased pre-processing and category erasure
  • Opaque data practices
  • Ethics dumping
  • Deploying AI systems trained without local, contextual data
  • Western-centric research infrastructures

‚úÖ High-Agency Pathways

  • Decentering Western ontologies
  • Taking a needs-based approach to developing AI
  • Early co-design and participatory initiatives
  • Establishing consent and contextually appropriate compensation
  • Creating culturally inclusive datasets
  • Community-engaged data production
  • Crowdsourcing data collection
  • Building public visibility in dataset development
  • Developing equitable data licensing models
  • Developing federated data spaces
  • Participatory data ownership and governance
